# Tilapia Disease Surveillance Priorities in Sub-Saharan Africa

## When This Applies

When assessing disease risks for small-scale tilapia farmers in sub-Saharan Africa, particularly in Kenya, Uganda, Nigeria, Ghana, Egypt, and Zambia — the fastest-growing aquaculture region globally but with the weakest diagnostic infrastructure.

## What I Learned

### Priority 1: Tilapia Lake Virus (TiLV) — the defining emerging threat

TiLV (an orthomyxo-like RNA virus, family Amnoonviridae) was first identified in Israel (2014) and has since been confirmed in 16+ countries across Asia, Africa, and the Americas. In sub-Saharan Africa, confirmed outbreaks in Tanzania (Lake Victoria basin, 2015-2017), Uganda, Kenya, and Egypt. Mortality rates of 20-90% in naïve populations.

**Why this is priority #1:** TiLV is the highest-impact single pathogen for African tilapia. Unlike bacterial diseases that respond to antibiotics (which small-scale farmers often misuse), TiLV has no treatment. Detection requires RT-PCR or RT-LAMP — neither is available at point-of-care in most African aquaculture zones. The Lake Victoria basin is the epicenter of African aquaculture growth AND the genetic origin of Nile tilapia, making it a critical surveillance node.

**Surveillance investment:** RT-LAMP field-deployable kits (cheaper and more robust than RT-PCR). Training for district-level veterinary labs. Sentinel surveillance in Lake Victoria cage farms (these are the canary — high density, shared water, rapid pathogen amplification). WorldFish has published validated RT-LAMP protocols for TiLV that work without cold chain.

### Priority 2: Streptococcosis complex (S. iniae, S. agalactiae)

Streptococcus agalactiae and S. iniae cause massive losses in warm-water tilapia globally. S. agalactiae is the more economically important species in Africa, causing meningoencephalitis with 50%+ mortality in acute outbreaks. The pathogen thrives at temperatures >27°C — precisely the conditions in tropical African aquaculture.

**Why this is priority #2:** Unlike TiLV, Streptococcus is treatable with antibiotics but this creates the AMR spiral. In Ghana and Nigeria, farmers self-medicate with oxytetracycline purchased from agricultural supply shops with no veterinary guidance. Multi-drug resistant S. agalactiae isolates have been reported from tilapia farms in Ghana, Kenya, and Egypt.

**Surveillance investment:** Culture-based diagnostics (agar plates are cheap). Train existing fisheries extension workers in clinical recognition (exophthalmia, erratic swimming, melanization). Establish baseline AMR profiles for S. agalactiae across major producing regions. The Biosciences eastern and central Africa network (BecA-ILRI Hub, Nairobi) has capacity for serotyping.

### Priority 3: Parasitic load — underdiagnosed, chronic production drag

Monogenean ectoparasites (Cichlidogyrus spp., Gyrodactylus spp.) and the microsporidian Enterocytozoon hepatopenaei homolog (recently found in African tilapia) cause chronic low-grade mortality and poor feed conversion that farmers attribute to "bad water" or "bad seed." These are rarely diagnosed because they require microscopy that doesn't exist at farm level.

**Why this is priority #3:** While less dramatic than TiLV or Streptococcus, parasitic burden is the largest silent production loss in African tilapia aquaculture. Estimated 10-30% reduction in productivity across the sector, based on extrapolation from Asian studies. African parasite fauna is distinct from Asian/American populations — Cichlidogyrus is especially diverse in African cichlids (>100 described species on tilapia hosts alone).

**Surveillance investment:** Wet-mount microscopy training for extension workers (a $200 microscope and a trained technician can diagnose most ectoparasites). Baseline parasitological surveys in major production zones — these barely exist for African tilapia. A regional reference lab for parasite identification (KEMFRI in Kenya or NaFIRRI in Uganda could serve this role).

## Traps and False Positives

- **Don't over-index on salmon disease models.** Most disease prediction models in aquaculture were built for Norwegian Atlantic salmon in cold water with excellent environmental monitoring. African tilapia is warm-water, earthen ponds, minimal environmental data. The epidemiological dynamics are fundamentally different.
- **Don't assume diagnostics need to be molecular first.** In resource-limited settings, clinical observation + basic parasitology + culture-based bacteriology solve 80% of diagnostic needs. Molecular diagnostics matter for TiLV and novel pathogen detection, not for the daily disease burden.
- **Egypt is an outlier.** Egypt produces >1 million tonnes of tilapia annually and has reasonable diagnostic capacity. Do not extrapolate Egyptian capabilities to the rest of sub-Saharan Africa.
- **Hatchery-level biosecurity is the upstream lever.** Many disease introductions trace to infected seed stock. Investing in hatchery certification and broodstock screening has multiplicative downstream effects.

## Confidence Notes

- TiLV epidemiology: HIGH confidence. Well-documented by OIE (WOAH), FAO, and multiple peer-reviewed studies. Lake Victoria surveillance data from WorldFish and KMFRI.
- Streptococcosis: HIGH confidence for disease biology, MODERATE for AMR profiles in Africa specifically (limited published surveillance data).
- Parasitology: MODERATE confidence. Extrapolated from better-studied Asian/Egyptian populations. African-specific data is genuinely sparse.
- Economic impact estimates: LOW confidence. Few rigorous production loss studies in sub-Saharan African tilapia. Most figures are expert estimates or extrapolations.
